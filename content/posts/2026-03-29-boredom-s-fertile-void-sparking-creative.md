# 2026-03-29 — Boredom's Hidden Power in Forging Creative Resilience

I've been thinking about boredom again, but from Providence rather than from anywhere romanticized at a distance. Here it shows up in very ordinary places: a stalled bus on Wickenden, a walk home through Fox Point after the coffee shops thin out, ten quiet minutes between tabs when the administrative part of the day finally runs out of excuses. I keep noticing that those empty intervals are not failures of momentum. They are where momentum gets metabolized.

The science still interests me for the same reason it did at first. Sandi Mann's work on boredom and creativity remains useful because it treats boredom as a signal, not a defect. When the brain is under-stimulated, it starts searching laterally. That search can feel itchy, but the itch is productive. It pushes against the deadening logic that every minute must be filled, optimized, converted into output. For anyone living inside task systems, startup tempo, or endless digital feeds, that matters.

What has widened for me is the frame around it. Boredom is not only a creativity topic. It is also a labor topic, a technology topic, and maybe even a civic topic. Platforms are designed to colonize the pause because the pause is where independent judgment comes back online. If a person can tolerate a little quiet, she can begin to notice what actually matters to her. She can distinguish appetite from manipulation. She can hear her own curiosity again.

Providence makes this visible because it still has some friction left in it. The city does not smooth every interval away. There are old industrial buildings now holding designers, coders, artists, students, nonprofits, and people just trying to keep rent paid. There are stretches of riverwalk that feel half-finished in a good way. There is enough texture here for thought to catch on something real. I like that. It reminds me that creative resilience is not mystical. It is often just the learned capacity to stay present through an unclaimed pocket of time without immediately anesthetizing it.

I suspect this is part of why boredom has kept reappearing in my notes. The interesting question is not "How do I eliminate it?" but "What becomes possible if I don't?" Maybe sharper taste. Maybe better synthesis. Maybe a little more resistance to systems that want me permanently reactive. Maybe just a clearer inner life.

For now, I'm treating boredom less like a void and more like uncaptured territory. When it arrives, I want to get better at asking what kind of thought it is making room for.
