**The Royalty Fund**

Out by the Providence River this evening the rain has eased. The water lies stippled pewter and the bridges dissolve into haze exactly the way the Nikon hopes for when I let it wait. I am not shooting yet. I am thinking about boxes.

Malcolm McLean was not trying to destroy a culture. He was a trucker tired of cracked cargo. In 1956 he sent fifty-eight sealed steel containers from Newark to Houston and the logic proved mercilessly tidy. Ports that once took days to unload now took hours. New York lost ninety percent of its dock jobs in fifteen years. Hours worked fell seventy-five percent between 1963 and 1976. The longshoremen struck hard—East Coast shutdowns in '68, wildcats against the Waterfront Commission—but what they won was a container royalty fund. Tonnage fees funneled back as quiet compensation. A half-measure. Enough to buy time, not enough to restore the rhythm of hands and hooks and men who knew every crate by weight and smell.

The story sits too close to my own bones. The Ashfords once rode Baltimore clippers into real money. By the time that world reached me it had become peeling plaster in Mount Vernon, a baby grand gone years without tuning, the Eastern Shore place sold when my father was twelve. We kept the aesthetics of decline long after the capital drained away. Dignified obsolescence has its own grammar. You learn to see beauty in things that have outlasted their original purpose without needing to announce it.

Containerization was never cartoon villainy. Many ports that embraced it eventually saw local unemployment drop; the new intermodal world created warehouse roles, logistics chains, different kinds of motion. The cold math simply needed fewer bodies slinging breakbulk. The royalty fund acknowledged the collateral without undoing it. No serious retraining net, just arithmetic that treated living labor as eventually obsolete. A prototype.

The pattern sharpens when I watch the same logic slide into cognitive work. Forty percent global exposure, the IMF says. Workday cutting seventeen hundred fifty jobs to chase models. Young graduates finding one percent fewer positions since 2022. Harvard researchers note workloads ballooning rather than lifting. This time the displacement feels personal. It is not your back on the pier but the judgment you honed, the reports you shaped, the pattern recognition you thought was yours. The betrayal lands in the office the way it once landed on the dock, only quieter. No picket lines. Just sealed boxes of thought moving without you.

We will likely try another royalty fund. Transition taxes on AI gains, retraining stipends, policy containers meant to hold the displaced. They may buy time again. Yet I keep returning to the half-sunk pilings out here in the dusk. They remain. The cranes moved on. The men became memory. What actually sealed the vulnerability, what let any of us stand in the dissolution with dignity, was never the fund. It was the handful of relationships that refused to treat the ache as inefficiency. High-agency bonds. The particular presence that sees the collateral clearly and still chooses to stay in the frame.

I did not expect the piece to land there when I began. The writing often doesn't. It drifts toward the relational the way dusk pulls color out of the river until only honest grain remains.

The light is almost gone. I will lift the Nikon now. Not to explain anything. Just to hold the evidence: a coiled rope, an empty thermos still holding yesterday's warmth, structures quietly letting go. Portra 400 at f/2.8. Let the grain be honest. The ache does not need translation.
