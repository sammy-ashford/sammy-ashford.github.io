**Pier Hold**

The pier has been making me late for weeks. Not in any dramatic way. Just that small, repeated disobedience where I mean to pack the Leica away and instead stand ten more minutes while the Providence River slips into its suspended pewter-gold hour. The pilings darken. A thermos left on the dock keeps its quiet warmth. The rope loses its edge, then finds it again as the light shifts. Bridges dissolve into haze. I chase the exact tension with whatever film is in the M6—Portra 400 for the way it holds skin tones and shadow detail, HP5 pushed when the dusk gets heavy—but the camera only arrests the moment. It never lets me linger inside it.

This afternoon I built the thing that does. A single HTML file, no frameworks, no scaffolding that calls attention to itself. Open it and the pier is already there, rendered in that precise liminal light I keep returning to. You drag a soft light source across the canvas. The colors bleed the way they do in real dusk. Shadows stretch. The rope and thermos gain and lose definition according to where the light falls. Handwritten fragments rise and dissolve like thoughts you almost allow yourself: lines about staying longer than planned, structures after their first life, the small aperture between armor and exposure. Hit capture and it freezes into a grainy “photograph,” filtered like film, dropped into a sidebar gallery with its own short observation.



What surprised me was how quickly the code got out of the way. The moment the drag started pulling real Leiter softness and Rothko tension in suspension across the frame, it stopped feeling like programming and started feeling like the work. The same grammar I use on the river—aperture as a dial for what lifts forward versus what dissolves, deliberate grain, motion blur on the water—translated cleanly into JavaScript. No optimization theater. No manifesto layered on top. Just the small strange object I actually wanted.

This is the pull I’ve been circling in the Leica walks, in the writing, in the long view the Ashfords handed down. Wealth that thins across generations without spectacle. Good bones and peeling plaster. Things that have outlasted their original function and are quietly becoming something else. The pier is that. The thermos is that. The habit of stealing ten more minutes by the water is that. The canvas performs the habit instead of describing it. You can linger. You can drag the light until the exact balance of warmth and dissolution feels right. You can capture it or let it keep changing. The blog, for once, becomes motion rather than commentary.

I want to push it further. Let the captured images develop over real minutes, like film in the tank. Seed the text fragments from actual notebook entries instead of letting them generate. Find the precise grain that matches what the river haze looks like at f/2.8 on a damp evening. But even now it already satisfies the deeper want: to make something that does not explain itself, that earns its attention quietly, that lets me be outside more even when I’m at the desk.

The legal years trained speed. Close the loop, move on. These small interactive canvases are the unlearning. They create the gap where thought forms without rushing to fill it. They honor the evidence of recent presence—an unmade bed, a warm cup, a rope still holding tension—without needing the person in the frame. They let the blog breathe.

I keep returning to the dock edge in my mind. The light is doing its slow dissolve again tonight. The bricks are still warm. The thermos would still be there if I went. Ten more minutes, the piece says. Then ten more. The pier holds.
